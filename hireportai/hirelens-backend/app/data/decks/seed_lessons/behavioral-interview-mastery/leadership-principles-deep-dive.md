## Concept

Leadership-principle deep dives (Amazon's bar-raiser style; analogues
at Meta, Google, and most large engineering orgs) probe the same story
for 5-10 minutes with successively sharper questions. The interviewer
is testing depth, honesty, and self-reflection — not the story
itself.

The pattern: candidate gives a STAR answer. Interviewer asks "what was
the trade-off?" → "what was the disagreement?" → "what would you do
differently?" → "what did the team learn?" → "did the outcome hold up
six months later?"

Each level peels back rehearsal. Candidates who only have a surface
story collapse around question 3.

## Production

Two patterns that hold up under deep dives:

1. **Pre-rehearse the second-order questions.** For every story you
   plan to use, write down: the trade-off, the disagreement, the
   regret, the long-term outcome. If any of those is a blank, the
   story isn't deep enough — pick a different one.
2. **Be honest about regrets.** "Nothing I would change" is the wrong
   answer. Surface a non-fatal regret with the lesson attached. The
   interviewer is grading whether you can think critically about your
   own work, not whether you were perfect.

Watch for these failure modes:

- **The hero narrative.** Story where the candidate single-handedly
  saves a project no one else could. Reads as either inaccurate or
  unable to credit the team.
- **The dodged disagreement.** "We all agreed quickly" — interviewer
  hears "I avoid conflict".
- **The vague metric.** "It worked great" — give a number.

## Examples

| Deep-dive question         | What it's grading                  |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------|
| What was the trade-off?    | Senior judgment under constraint   |
| Who disagreed?             | Conflict navigation, intellectual honesty |
| What would you do differently? | Self-reflection                |
| Six months later?          | Long-term thinking, follow-through |
| What did the team learn?   | Force-multiplication beyond the project |

The skill is choosing 6-10 stories deep enough to survive 10 minutes
of probing each, then mapping them to leadership principles before the
interview rather than during.
