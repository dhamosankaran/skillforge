## Concept

A resume isn't a job log; it's an argument. The argument is "I can do
this next role". The strongest arguments are made through arcs — the
implicit story of how scope, complexity, and ownership grew across
roles.

When two candidates have similar bullets, the one whose arc reads
"successively bigger and harder problems" wins. When the arc reads
"sideways for 5 years", even strong individual bullets read as
"competent but plateaued".

## Production

Three arc patterns that work:

1. **Scope arc.** Owned a feature → owned a service → owned a domain.
   Showcase the level shift in the bullet structure: earlier roles emphasize
   craft, later roles emphasize trade-offs and partnerships.
2. **Depth arc.** Generalist → specialist. The same role family with
   increasing technical depth. Compelling for staff/principal pivots.
3. **Inflection arc.** Career-defining moment — a 0→1 product, a
   migration that changed the company, a system that's still in
   production. The story to bring to interviews.

Position the arc with the role-summary line. Two-line summary at the
top of each role:

> Senior Software Engineer, payments platform.
> Led the migration from a single-account model to a multi-tenant
> ledger that now processes $4B/yr.

That summary line is the thesis the bullets evidence.

## Examples

| Pattern                  | Reads as                                  |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Same level, 4 roles      | Plateau or job-hopping                    |
| Up one level per role    | Strong, expected trajectory               |
| Up two levels in 18 mo   | Either rocketship or title inflation      |
| Sideways then ICs to mgr | Re-positioned; tell that story explicitly |

The skill is choosing the arc you want to argue and pruning bullets
that don't serve it. A bullet that's true but irrelevant to the thesis
is dead weight. Cut it.
